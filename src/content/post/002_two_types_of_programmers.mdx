There two primary relationships a programmer holds towards to their
craft. The first is programming a means to an end. The second is
programming as an end within itself.

Those disposed to the former category of thought are more willing to
embrace AI tooling. They are more comfortable yielding control to the
machine, so long as the machine produces what they want. Sometimes
what these people want is _exclusively_ the end product. Whether or
not the code producing their end product is an entangled hair ball is
a matter of indifference. We call these people "vibe-coders."

The later category, perhaps out of insecurity that machines have
aggressively infringed on their economic and social niche (which it
has), tend towards a more reactionary rejection of AI programming as a
whole. Code which is clear, obvious and uses the right data strucutres
forms [a well recieved philosophy](). Unforuntately this is precisely
the philosophy which makes idiomatic code precisely in-distribution of
language models. When writing natural language, mode-collapse produces
unstyalized, lifeless crap. When writing software, mode-collapse is
what we call "idiomatic". I would concede that the idiomatic code is
in fact not the mode - the mode is verbose crappy boiler plate - but
it _is in distribution_, and can be easily invoked through a modicum
of intention.

Somewhere in between is what I see more people calling _Agentic
Engineering_. This term is not vibe-coder copium. It is a distinction,
a middle ground between the ludite and the viber. The distinction is
simple, Agentic Engineers recognize and care about code quality,
vibe-coders don't.

Riddiculing AI is clearly the ludite perspective. I have a lot of
sympathy for the ludite perspective. Unfortunately, programmers since
the inception of the profession have, and will continue, producing
better, more sophisticated abstractions for producing code.
[Programming as an Identity]() has been dealt a crushing blow.
Engineering has always been about reduction of known practices. "Known
practices" is in the name. AI's are good at doing things which are
known. You just need to tell them.

Job loss is a somewhat orthogonal discussion. I've seen [Jevons
Paradox] being discussed several times as the non-doomer case for
programmer employment post AI. What I can not forsee, however, is
humans writing code by hand in any capacity whatsoever. Natural
language is the final, highest level abstraction of programming. It is
the final achievement, and all further improvements to code-producing
abstractions will take place within agentic frameworks, which impose
some genre of discipline to condition the generation. The human will
always have the option to be in the loop, but they may not need to be.

And of course, different types of software merit their own
"automatability score." It seems quite obvious to me that anything at
the application layer will be thoroughly automated by the end of this
year. The deployment platforms are becoming _so frictionless and
scalable it is astouning_. Software lower in the software stack has a
critical mid-section that is different.

I use claude-code for fleshing out my research atop of Mozillas
SpiderMonkey. If I went ahead with the first working version of the
AOT mode I developed, which was horrendously ugly, `#ifdef` mess which
bifurcated all of codegen, simply because it worked, there is no doubt
it would be classified as a failure case of AI. However, just like
writing software pre-AI, the discovery of the optimial compression,
representation and abstraction is an iterative process. I prompt,
scrutinize, compress, repeat until the thing is so simple that if fits
in my head. My role has been reduced to nitpiking patterns,
identifying complexity, testing, and debugging. The AI only produces
slop if we lower our standards. The laziness that is associated with
AI programming producing shit code is a correlated, but not causal
property of the tool. And I admit to being lazy many a time,
maintaining discipline is difficult while opening Pandora's box. But
it's something I'm learning to improve.

Everyone has an opinion on the internet these days. A common criticism
I see circulating is: "if AI is so productive, where are all the cool
things you have built?". This is a valid criticism. Billions of tokens
flow through models each day, but most AI enthusiasts show up empty
handed for all their evangelical endorsements. I attribute this not to
AI's being poor tools for code generation, but frankly that that _most
peoples ideas suck_. There is also a very real, very concerning
phenomenon of psychohancy creating [AI dellusion](). Am I dellusional
too? The only way to know is by bumping into reality.

Words are cheap. Actions have cost. To put some skin the game, and
take the risk of looking like a fool (if I don't already appear as
one), I'm going to be developing in public. On Tuesday March 17th,
2026 I had the idea for [FrictionBox](https://frictionbox.app) while
in the shower. For a few hours every evening I developed it entirely
with Claude code Max, running 2-3 agents in parallel on average.
Within one week I have a chrome extension published, Android app
pending, 12 micro-apps in the store - an entirely viable MVP. I'm now
squarely in the position of *does my idea suck like the rest of them*,
which, as far as I'm concerned, is exactly where I should be. I've had
many terrible ideas before, I wouldn't be suprised if this is another,
but I'm going to give it a shot.
